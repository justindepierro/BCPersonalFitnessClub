üèÉ Raw Splits & Timing

Mass (kg) = Weight (lb) √ó 0.4536

Body mass converted to kilograms. Used in every physics calculation ‚Äî force, momentum, and power all depend on mass. Heavier athletes produce more force/momentum but may be slower.

0‚Äì20 (s) Raw Freelap split

Time from start to 20-yard mark. This is the acceleration / drive phase ‚Äî the most important phase for football because plays begin from a stop or near-stop. Includes reaction and first-step quickness. Lower is better.

20‚Äì30 (s) Raw Freelap split

Time for the 10 yards from the 20 to 30 mark. The transition phase ‚Äî athlete is shifting from acceleration mechanics (forward lean) to upright top-end sprinting. Lower is better.

30‚Äì40 (s) Raw Freelap split

Time for the final 10 yards. The top-speed / max-velocity phase. If this is slower than the 20‚Äì30 split, the athlete is decelerating ‚Äî a flag for speed endurance or mechanics breakdown. Lower is better.

40 Total = (0‚Äì20) + (20‚Äì30) + (30‚Äì40)

Sum of all three splits. The traditional NFL Combine benchmark for straight-line speed. Graded against HS standards by position group. Lower is better.

‚ö° Velocity (Speed)

v1 (m/s) = 18.288 m √∑ t0‚Äì20

Average velocity over the first 20 yards (18.288 meters). Tells you how fast they're moving on average during the drive phase. Higher is better. Typical HS range: 5.5 ‚Äì 7.5 m/s.

v2 (m/s) = 9.144 m √∑ t20‚Äì30

Average velocity over the 20‚Äì30 yard segment (9.144 meters). Should be higher than v1 if the athlete is still accelerating. Typical HS range: 7.0 ‚Äì 9.5 m/s.

v3 (m/s) = 9.144 m √∑ t30‚Äì40

Average velocity over the final 10-yard segment. This is the closest measure to true top-end speed. If v3 < v2, the athlete is slowing down. Typical HS range: 7.0 ‚Äì 9.8 m/s.

vMax = max(v1, v2, v3)

The highest velocity recorded across all three segments ‚Äî the athlete's raw speed ceiling. Almost always v2 or v3 unless there's timing error. Graded against HS standards.

v10 Best = max(v2, v3)

Best 10-yard split velocity. Removes the 20-yard acceleration segment (which includes starting from rest) to give the purest top-end speed comparison. Graded against HS standards.

Top MPH = vMax √ó 2.237

Top speed converted to miles per hour ‚Äî the most intuitive speed reference for coaches and players. An 8.0 m/s vMax ‚âà 17.9 mph; 9.0 m/s ‚âà 20.1 mph. For context, elite NFL combine sprinters reach ~22-23 mph. Not separately graded (same ranking as vMax).

üöÄ Acceleration

a1 (m/s¬≤) = v1 √∑ t0‚Äì20

Average acceleration during the drive phase (from standstill). This is the first-step explosiveness metric ‚Äî critical for linemen off the snap, WRs off the line, DBs breaking on the ball. Higher is better. Note: this uses average velocity, so the true kinematic acceleration is approximately 2√ó this value. The simplified formula keeps all athletes on the same scale for comparison.

a2 = (v2 ‚àí v1) √∑ t20‚Äì30

Rate of velocity change during transition. Positive = still speeding up. Near-zero means plateauing. This is color-coded: green = positive, red = negative (decelerating).

a3 = (v3 ‚àí v2) √∑ t30‚Äì40

Rate of velocity change at top speed. Negative values are common ‚Äî most HS athletes decelerate here. A large negative flags poor speed endurance or mechanical breakdown. Color-coded like a2.

üí™ Force (Newtons)

F1 (N) = mass (kg) √ó a1

Average horizontal force during the drive phase. This is the "how hard they push off the ground" number. Combines size AND acceleration ‚Äî a 200 lb athlete who accelerates well will have a very high F1. Graded against HS standards. Higher is better.

F2 = mass (kg) √ó a2

Average horizontal force during transition. Usually small or slightly positive ‚Äî athlete is near top speed. Color-coded: green if positive, red if negative.

F3 = mass (kg) √ó a3

Average horizontal force during last 10 yards. Often negative ‚Äî meaning the athlete is decelerating (net braking force). Very negative values indicate the athlete's sprint mechanics or conditioning break down at top speed.

üöÇ Momentum & Power

p‚Çë (kg¬∑m/s) = mass (kg) √ó v10 Best

Peak Momentum ‚Äî the "freight train" number and the "how hard to tackle" metric. Combines mass and top-end speed. A 100 kg lineman at 8 m/s (p‚Çë = 800) is harder to stop than a 70 kg WR at 9 m/s (p‚Çë = 630). Graded against HS standards.

p1 (kg¬∑m/s) = mass (kg) √ó v1

Momentum at the end of the 0‚Äì20 yard phase. How much force an opponent would need to stop them after their initial burst. Different from p‚Çë ‚Äî this captures momentum after acceleration, not at top speed.

P1 (W) = F1 √ó v1

Sprint Power during drive phase. Mechanical power output = force √ó velocity. Higher values mean the athlete is producing high force WHILE moving fast. The ultimate acceleration performance metric.

P2 = F2 √ó v2

Sprint power during transition. Because a2 (and thus F2) is often small, P2 is usually much lower than P1 ‚Äî the athlete is fast but not accelerating much. Color-coded.

P3 = F3 √ó v3

Sprint power in the max-velocity phase. Usually low or negative (deceleration). If positive, the athlete is still gaining speed in the last 10 yards ‚Äî rare and impressive at the HS level. Color-coded.

üìê Math Notes & Methodology

Distance conversions: 20 yards = 18.288 m, 10 yards = 9.144 m (1 yard = 0.9144 m)

Acceleration model: Uses a simplified a‚ÇÅ = v_avg / t formula. Under true constant-acceleration kinematics from rest, a = 2d/t¬≤ (double the reported value). We use the simplified model because (a) it's standard practice in HS/college field sprint testing, (b) real sprints aren't constant-acceleration, and (c) the simplified values are internally consistent ‚Äî every athlete is compared on the same scale, so rankings and grades are valid even though the absolute Newton values are field estimates rather than lab-grade.

What drives each metric: Velocity metrics reward pure speed. Force metrics reward the combination of size + acceleration (favors bigger athletes who move well). Momentum favors mass √ó speed. Power favors the rare athlete who is both forceful AND fast.

Derived Strength & Power

Peak power (Sayers formula), relative strength ratios, and sprint-strength utilisation. All 1RM values are tested maxes; derived metrics combine them with body mass and vertical jump.

üìê Sayers Peak Power: Ppeak = 60.7 √ó VJcm + 45.3 √ó masskg ‚àí 2055  |  Strength Utilisation: F1 / (Squatkg √ó g) ‚Äî how much max strength is expressed during sprint acceleration.
üìñ Column Reference Guide ‚Äî What Every Column Means

üèãÔ∏è Raw Strength Measures

Wt (lb) Raw measured weight

Body weight in pounds as recorded during testing. Contextualizes all other metrics ‚Äî a 300 lb bench means something very different for a 150 lb WR vs a 250 lb lineman. Also used to compute relative strength ratios.

Mass (kg) = Weight (lb) √ó 0.4536

Body mass in kilograms. Required for physics-based calculations (Sayers Peak Power, sprint force, momentum). The international standard unit for mass in all biomechanics equations.

Bench (lb) Bench Press 1RM (tested max)

Maximum weight the athlete can press for one rep on the flat bench press. The primary measure of upper-body pressing strength. Important for blocking (OL), shedding blocks (DL), and tackling power (LB). Graded against HS standards. Higher is better.

Squat (lb) Back Squat 1RM (tested max)

Maximum weight in the barbell back squat. The gold-standard measure of lower-body maximal strength. Foundational for sprinting (drive phase), jumping, and change of direction. Strongly correlated with sprint acceleration (a1) and 40-yard times. Graded against HS standards. Higher is better.

‚öñÔ∏è Relative Strength (Pound-for-Pound)

Rel Bench = Bench 1RM (lb) √∑ Body Weight (lb)

Upper-body strength relative to body weight. A value of 1.0 means the athlete can bench their own body weight ‚Äî a solid baseline for HS athletes. Values above 1.3 are excellent. Allows fair comparison across weight classes. Graded against HS standards.

Rel Squat = Squat 1RM (lb) √∑ Body Weight (lb)

Lower-body strength relative to body weight. This is one of the strongest predictors of athletic performance ‚Äî research shows it correlates with sprint times, vertical jump, and change of direction. A value of 1.5+ is strong at the HS level; 2.0+ is elite. Graded against HS standards.

üí• Explosive Power Measures

VJ (in) Countermovement Vertical Jump (tested)

Standing vertical jump height in inches. Measures lower-body explosive power ‚Äî the ability to generate force rapidly. Used directly in the Sayers Peak Power formula. Key metric for skill positions (WR, DB) and any athlete who needs to elevate. Graded against HS standards. Higher is better.

Peak Power (W) = 60.7 √ó VJcm + 45.3 √ó masskg ‚àí 2055

Sayers Peak Power ‚Äî a validated estimate of lower-body peak mechanical power output (in Watts). Combines vertical jump height with body mass. Published by Sayers et al. (1999) and widely used in S&C research. Higher values mean the athlete can produce more force rapidly. Graded against HS standards.

Rel PP (W/kg) = Peak Power (W) √∑ mass (kg)

Relative Peak Power ‚Äî power output per kilogram of body mass. Normalizes for size, so a lighter athlete who jumps high can score equally to a heavier athlete. Important for positions where you need to move your own body quickly (WR, DB, RB). Graded against HS standards.

MB (in) Seated Med Ball Throw (tested)

Distance of a seated medicine ball chest pass (in inches). Measures upper-body explosive power ‚Äî the ability to generate force rapidly through the arms and chest. Correlates with hitting power, stiff-arm ability, and throwing velocity. Complementary to bench press: bench measures max strength, MB measures how fast you can apply it. Graded against HS standards.

MB Rel = MB Throw (in) √∑ Body Weight (lb)

Med Ball throw distance relative to body weight. Measures explosive power efficiency ‚Äî how much "pop" you get per pound. Helps identify athletes who are explosive for their size vs. athletes who throw far purely because they're big. Graded against HS standards.

üîó Sprint-Strength Link

Strength Util = F1 √∑ (Squatkg √ó 9.81)

Strength Utilisation ratio ‚Äî asks "how much of this athlete's maximal strength is actually expressed during sprinting?" F1 (sprint force) is divided by the theoretical max force from the squat (squat weight in kg √ó gravity). A low value means the athlete is strong in the weight room but isn't translating it to sprint acceleration ‚Äî a coaching flag for sprint mechanics, rate of force development, or neural activation. Typical range: 0.05 ‚Äì 0.20. Not formally graded ‚Äî used as a diagnostic tool.

üìê Formulas & Methodology

Sayers Formula: Ppeak = 60.7 √ó VJ(cm) + 45.3 √ó mass(kg) ‚àí 2055. Published by Sayers et al. (1999), validated against force-plate measurements. Requires both vertical jump and body mass. VJ is converted from inches to cm (√ó 2.54), weight from lb to kg (√ó 0.4536).

Relative measures: Dividing by body weight (lb-to-lb for bench/squat/MB) creates a fair comparison across body sizes. A 150 lb athlete benching 195 lb (Rel 1.30) is performing at the same relative level as a 220 lb athlete benching 286 lb (Rel 1.30).

Strength Utilisation context: Because sprint F1 uses the simplified kinematic model (see Sprint Analysis tab), the absolute Strength Util numbers are relative ‚Äî not directly interpretable as a "percentage of max strength." But the ranking across athletes is valid: higher = better strength transfer to sprinting.
